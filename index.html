A standard reporting layer for energy systems

Write energy as: 1 MWh, f_X = 0.73.

That is the adoption-friendly version of the framework: keep the familiar energy quantity, then add one quality number. A megawatt-hour of electricity, a megawatt-hour of 80 C heat, and a megawatt-hour of 40 C heat are not equally useful, so every reported energy quantity should carry an Exergy Factor.

1 MWh, f_X = 0.73 1 kWh, f_X = 0.73 accessible = 0.73 MWh_ex
Work-potential ladder

The implication

Quantity tells you scale. f_X tells you fit.

Conventional energy accounting answers the first-law question: how many joules, kilowatt-hours, barrels, or megawatt-hours moved? That is necessary, but incomplete. Energy systems also need the second-law question: how much useful work potential did that stream carry at the point where it mattered?

Exergy Factor makes that second question operational. The notation is intentionally plain: write 1 MWh, f_X = 0.73 instead of reporting 1 MWh alone. Examples use values below 1 so readers immediately see that energy quantity and useful work potential are different.

What changes in practice

  • Heat recovery can rank streams by temperature and sink, not heat quantity alone.
  • District energy can publish both MWh and MWh_ex delivered.
  • Fuel comparisons can stop mixing LHV, HHV, and chemical-exergy claims.
  • Storage can be compared by service grade, not just nameplate MWh.
  • Audits can report savings as both energy saved and work potential avoided.

Adoption pattern

Make quantity, f_X = value the default energy record.

1

Start with the normal energy number

Use the same MWh, kWh, MW, GJ, therm, or fuel-basis unit already used in the project.

2

Add f_X = ...

Attach accessible exergy per unit energy, such as 1 MWh, f_X = 0.73.

3

Declare the context

Record the reference sink, reporting boundary, and operating basis so the factor is interpretable.

Thermal example

Compute a heat-stream Exergy Factor.

For a heat source and sink, the thermal Exergy Factor is the Carnot factor. This turns temperature measurements that many facilities already collect into a reporting-quality field.

Drop the reporting format into existing data pipelines.

Minimal Python use

from quantity_quality import EnergyReport, ReferenceContext
from quantity_quality import thermal_exergy_factor_c

context = ReferenceContext(
    reference="20 C sink",
    boundary="district heating delivery",
    operating_basis="Carnot heat factor"
)

f_x = thermal_exergy_factor_c(80, 20)
report = EnergyReport(1.0, "MWh", f_x, context)
print(report.as_dict())
